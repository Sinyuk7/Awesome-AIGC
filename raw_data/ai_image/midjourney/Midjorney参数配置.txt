A Style Reference (the `--sref` parameter) is a way to capture the visual vibe of an existing image and apply it to your new Midjourney creations. There are two ways to use it: with an **Image** (Midjourney or external) or a **code**. Format like this:

`--sref <URL>`

`--sref 1234` or `--sref random`

## **What is `--sref`? (style reference)**

</aside>

A Style Reference is a way to capture the visual vibe of an existing image and apply it to your new Midjourney creations. It doesn't copy objects or people, just the overall style—like colors, medium, textures, or lighting—helping you achieve a consistent visual theme.

The magic of using a "style reference" image (--sref) is that Midjourney will strive to pick up only the *aesthetic* qualities of a reference image instead of its semantic content as well. In this sense, we can think of --sref like a text expander type shortcut.  Think about the prompt that created the fox image. Because of --sref, for my *new* image I won't have to retype "A textured, impasto, expressive oil painting depicting" or "The painting is rendered in a mixture of teals and blacks"  or “The mood is suspenseful and mysterious" because in a sense it will be *done for me* by Midjourney.
    
    **Importantly, the prompt to create the new canvas has no aesthetic content (adjectives) of its own. This helps sref perform!**
    
    I will use the fox image as my --sref to provide the aesthetic content, instead.
    
    ```
    A young man stands at the edge of the forest cliff, looking over the ocean below. --sref https://s.mj.run/9DFZsjKwkyE --v 6.1
    ```

    If you're interested in using multiple style reference images, place all URLs after --sref separated by spaces, like this:
    `A  young man stands at the edge of the forest cliff, looking over the ocean below. --sref <https://url.com/earth-style.png> <https://url.com/wind-style.png> <https://url.com/fire-style.png> <https://url.com/air-style.png>  --v 6.1`
    
    **➡️  Sidebar: Multiple URLs with Weights**
    
    If you're interested in making one of the style reference images more dominant than the others, give it a weight that is relative to the weight of the other URLs.
    `A  young man stands at the edge of the forest cliff, looking over the ocean below. --sref <https://url.com/earth-style.png::2> <https://url.com/wind-style.png::1> --v 6.1`
    In this example, earth-style is twice as influential as wind-style. As with weights everywhere in Midjourney, the exact numbers aren't in play,  just the relationship between them. You could just as easily use ::1.0 and ::0.5 or ::100 and ::50.
    
    1. **Optionally, clean up any undesirable details.**
    
    Note Midjourney applied ONLY the aesthetics and NOT the content. There's no fox, right? But in some cases, Midjourney will also pick up *some* of the content, like the night sky with moon, which my new prompt didn't specify. It comes from the `sref` image. Oops.
    
    If you want to get away from the style reference image accidentally adding undesirable details to your canvas, there are three methods you can explore.
    
    **Three methods to explore:**
    
    1. Modify the prompt.
        - *Too many semantic details?* Write a prompt that *replaces* the undesirable details with ones that you do find desirable.
        - *Not enough of the style?* Modify your prompt to *lean in* to the style you're chasing. If you want `textured, impasto, expressive oil painting` and it is not showing up strongly enough, add the words to the prompt in solidarity with your sref images.
    2. Change your `-sw` from the default of 100.
        - *Too many semantic details?* Lower your `-sw` from the default of 100 to something less dominating, like `-sw 20`.
        - *Not enough of the style?* Raise your `-sw` from the default of 100 to something more dominating, like `-sw 800` (max is 1000).
    3. Use small weights to emphasize or de-emphasize elements on the canvas you find undesirable.
    
    **In this example, I lowered the --sw value:**
    
    ```
    A man stands in a parking lot of futuristic cars against a neon cyberpunk cityscape. --sref https://s.mj.run/9DFZsjKwkyE --v 6.0 --sw 22
    ```

### **How do I use sref** codes**?**

</aside>

There are two ways to use sref codes! 

1. Use the sref parameter + type any whole number between `0` and `4294967295`; format like this: `--sref 123`. If you choose a number higher than is available, it’ll roll over the count.
2. Let Midjourney return a random number for you by replacing the number with “random” like this: `--sref random`.
    - The random style will be returned as a numeric code, which you can then re-use with any prompt to roughly recreate that style.
    - ⚠️ `random` does not work in v6.0, only v6.1 and v7

### Can I combine srefs? Can I weight them individually?

</aside>

**You sure can! Here are some options …**

- Blend multiple sref codes:  `--sref 123 456`
- Mine multiple random codes at once: `--sref random random` .
- Blend images & numbers: `--sref 123 456 https://www.host.com/image.png`
- Note: the order does not matter.

**Also, try changing the influence of each sref using standard weighting syntax …**

- Weighted numbers: `--sref 123::1 456::2` (Discord + Web)
- Weighted number and image: `--sref 123::0.5 456::1 https://www.host.com/image.png::0.5`
- *Weights may include integers, decimals, and negative numbers*.

## What are sref versions (--sv) ?

</aside>

Each style reference ***version*** offers a unique way of interpreting and applying your Style Reference, so experiment and see what works best for you! Note the V7 and V6 differences below.

### V7 sref versions

There are 6 versions total. --sv 6 is default, and --sv 4 is the old V7 sref model (prior to June 16, 2025). Sref random and sref codes are only compatible with --sv 4 and 6.

## **What are sref weights (--sw) ?**

</aside>

You can control the influence of the style reference with the `--sw` parameter, where `--sw 1` is the minimum, `--sw 1000` is the maximum, and `--sw 100` is default.

**Pertaining to 'sref' weights, could you tell me the minimum/maximum weight range? Also, do they have to be expressed in whole numbers, or can one use decimal values for a lighter effect (e.g., .25, .5, etc.)?**

They work like other weights in Midjourney. They are only compared to each other. They are proportions in relationship to each other so that a weight of 1.0 and a weight of 0.5 is the same as a weight of 100 and a weight of 50. So there's no minimum or maximum value.  They are relational numbers. They can be expressed as decimals but there may be rounding on the back-end. (this is so users can type anything they want and still get approximately the effect they had in mind without throwing an error message.)


# Parameter: --style raw / --raw

<aside>
<img src="/icons/magic-wand_gray.svg" alt="/icons/magic-wand_gray.svg" width="40px" />

### Overview (TL;DR)

*Styles* are prepackaged configurations of Midjourney that change the way our prompts are expressed on the canvas. In v6 & v7, there are two Styles available: **Standard** (aka “default” or “house style”) and **Raw**. 

You can think of Raw as a "skinny" version of Midjourney … it skips the aesthetic overlay or “post-production” work that Standard includes. It may appear to create more prompt adherence, and it may improve realistic quality of photographs when paired with terms like *photograph of*.

Try it by typing the parameter: `--style raw` or `--raw` ; or by activating the setting in Discord or web (FYI it’s called Raw *Mode* instead of *Style* 🤷‍♀️)

</aside>

## What is the `--style raw` or `--raw` parameter?

</aside>

When we don't specify a `--style`, we're using the Midjourney default house style (plus an `--sref` if we include one). If we *do* specify a `--style`, we're *replacing* the default house style with our choice.

### Is `--style raw` the same as `--raw`?

</aside>

Yes, but it saves your fingers from having to type 6 characters! 😇

<aside>
<img src="/icons/help-alternate_blue.svg" alt="/icons/help-alternate_blue.svg" width="40px" />

### How does it work?

</aside>

As with all `--style` parameters, you can increase the influence of "`--style raw`" with the value of "`--s N`" (stylize) where the value of "`N`" can be between 0 and 1000. If you don't specify `--s` (stylize) the value will be 100.

Midjourney's "house" or "default" style has *amazing* intelligence around aesthetics. Some of that intelligence is tucked away in the black box of Midjourney's inner workings. But other elements of Midjourney's aesthetic intelligence are open for us to configure in the form of parameters like:

- style (`--style`) allows us to choose different configurations of the black box
- stylize (`--s`) allows us to increase/decrease the influence of the style we've chosen
- sref (`--sref`) allows us to pick up and use an aesthetic from a style reference image provided with a URL
- sref weight (`--sw`) allows us to increase/decrease the influence of the sref we've chosen
- chaos (`--c`) adds a random element so that each image in the grid is different
- weird (`--w`) steers the images in the grid away from whatever aesthetic appears to be trending on the gallery

When you use "`--style raw`" in your prompt, you are loading up a configuration that does a little less "post-production work" for you.  Some people say that if your prompt asks for a photograph, it will look more realistic using `--style raw`.

As with all `--styles`, the effect is *complex* so it is hard to nail down in a single statement. It appears to me to use less memory than the default house style. It is a "skinny" version of Midjourney, if you will. It does less. That's why using `--style raw` might appear to create more prompt adherence. You can achieve a similar adherence using any other style if you lower stylize (`--s`) to a value below 100, because that also takes up a bit less memory. (At the cost of coherency and beauty.)

⚠️ Be aware that using "`--style raw`" also means that if your prompt is *missing*  words that tell Midjourney how to create the image (like `photographic` or `rendered as video game concept art`), the output may be less visually appealing or coherent. To troubleshoot, you can experiment with decreasing or increasing the value of "`--s`" (stylize).

### When might I use `--style raw`?

</aside>

- If you want the aesthetic phrases *you've* crafted to be a leading influence, and lowering stylize (`--s`) didn't help, you might explore using `--style raw`.

- For photographs in particular, if you would like to see a less "post-processed" type of photograph, you might explore `--style raw` to reduce that post-processed look. Make sure to include cues in the prompt, too.
`a pride of lions rests in the shade of a tree, amateur snapshot photography --style raw`

</aside>

No. Adding words like `photographic, photograph, photography` works. `Raw` doesn't *create* photographs, it's just *useful* when making photographs because it can reduce the "post-processing" look that some people find a little uncanny.  As always, some prompts respond differently than others.

## What does `--style raw` do in Midjourney video?

</aside>

Raw behaves a little differently when used for images and video (animations)…

- **For Images:** Raw mode does less post-production work than Standard mode (strips away the default house style)
- **For Video (Animation):** by default, your animation’s prompt passes through an LLM layer which optimizes it for video output. When you are in Raw mode, your prompt is run exactly as written.

# **Parameter: --p (Profiles, Moodboards, Ranking, Personalization)**

<aside>

### Overview (TL;DR)

Personalization is a way to teach Midjourney your visual preferences, and store these preferences as alphanumeric codes that you add to your prompt. This is also referred to as Profiles due to the parameter `--profile` (`--p` for short). Make your Personalization/Profiles by ranking images (for Global Profile and Ranked Profiles) or by creating a collection of 1-500 images of your choosing (for Moodboards).

## **What is Personalization? what are Profiles?**

</aside>

Personalization (Profiles) acts like a style assistant for your image creations. By liking or ranking images and creating moodboards, Midjourney learns your visual preferences and uses that information to generate images that match your tastes and unique style. Personalization is compatible with Midjourney versions 6.0, 6.1, 7, and Niji 6. 

We have three types:

- **Global Profiles**
- **Ranked Profiles**
- **Moodboard Profiles.**

We use the profile parameter (`--p`) to create images using our own aesthetic preferences.

### **How do I make a Global or Ranked Profile?**

</aside>

- Your default or Global Profile is generated when you rank/rate a minimum number of images by choosing "Create Standard Profile" @ https://www.midjourney.com/personalize
- The Global Profile is used as the default, and included automatically if you use `--p` by itself in a prompt.
- You can also create additional Ranked profiles (built from ranking image pairs) which you can reference with `--p code`.
- **How do I “rank” and “like” images for my profile?**
    
      Ranking:
    
    - You need a specific number of rankings for each Midjourney Version, for the basic default/global profile to "kick in."
    - You can see how many rankings you have with `/info`.
    - Rank on the web here  https://www.midjourney.com/personalize
    - Select your personal aesthetic preference from a pair of images.
    - *Ignore the prompt.*
    - If you don't like either of the choices, use the "skip" button.
    - Both ranking and *some* "likes" (s) are incorporated into your personalization profile.

    - Go to https://www.midjourney.com/explore
    - Hover over the images you like best and select the heart emoji.

    - Review and refine your collection of "liked" images by using the [Likes] tab or by visiting https://www.midjourney.com/explore?tab=likes.

### **What are Moodboards?**

</aside>

- Moodboards are `--p` profiles made from images you add, instead of images you rank.
- Moodboards are `--p` (profiles), not multi-image style references. That is, they are not the same thing as using `--sref URL URL URL`. Style reference strives to draw from visual style only, while `--p` (profiles) also pull composition, color, subject matter, visual conventions, detail level, motifs, vibes, aesthetics, etc.
- The variety of input images directly affects output variation: similar images produce uniform styles, while diverse images yield far more complex, blended, or eclectic outputs.
- Currently, moo bards max cap at 500 images.
- You can only delete images from moodboards from the desktop. Hover over the image you want to delete and look for the trash can icon.
- You may need to refresh the moodboard to see additions to it.

<aside>

### When should I use a mood board versus a profile?

</aside>

**Mood boards feel a little like mini training sessions, helping Midjourney lock onto the aesthetic you're chasing.** The fewer images you include, the more focused the result. The more visually similar those images are, the more consistent the style will be. The reverse is also true. Eclectic mood board = eclectic output. While the prompt will always be the most powerful force in Midjourney, mood boards are surprisingly assertive in the blend, often exerting more influence than style references (`--sref`) or profiles (`--p`).

**Profiles, on the other hand, might feel  more like a steady undercurrent.** They shape your image from beneath the surface, quietly guiding the aesthetic. They do blend with other style modifiers, but their influence may feel deep or subtle. Where mood boards can feel shouty, profiles are more like a persistent gravitational pull. They are stable, refined, and consistent. Think of them as a reliable foundation rather than a dramatic flair. 

> **Profiles whisper. Mood boards yell.**
> 

Use profiles when you want a consistent aesthetic undercurrent. 

Use mood boards when you want to grab Midjourney by the collar and say, “Paint it like *this*. *This* is the vibe. *This* right here.”

## **What are sref weights (--sw) ?**

</aside>

You can control the influence of the style reference with the `--sw` parameter, where `--sw 1` is the minimum, `--sw 1000` is the maximum, and `--sw 100` is default.

**Pertaining to 'sref' weights, could you tell me the minimum/maximum weight range? Also, do they have to be expressed in whole numbers, or can one use decimal values for a lighter effect (e.g., .25, .5, etc.)?**

They work like other weights in Midjourney. They are only compared to each other. They are proportions in relationship to each other so that a weight of 1.0 and a weight of 0.5 is the same as a weight of 100 and a weight of 50. So there's no minimum or maximum value.  They are relational numbers. They can be expressed as decimals but there may be rounding on the back-end. (this is so users can type anything they want and still get approximately the effect they had in mind without throwing an error message.)

### Overview (TL;DR)

Omni-reference (or `--oref`) helps you get roughly the same character, animal, building, or object etc across jobs. Midjourney uses a semantic understanding of the image to transfer attributes of it. ## **What is Omni-reference (--oref)?**

</aside>

Omni-reference (or `--oref`) helps you get roughly the same character or object across jobs. It allows you to stabilize not just characters, but also… anything? Everything? Think buildings, animals, distinct objects, unique shapes.

Basically, the `--oref` parameter lets you create a subject based on an image you provide via URL (or attachment, if you use the web).  It’s helpful for keeping visual consistency, especially if the prompt includes some stabilizing or anchoring phrases. But, be aware, at time of writing, it can’t be used in the Full Editor (because that’s still v6.1 behind the curtain).

<aside>
💡

The promptcraft challenge with `oref`: Midjourney is semantically aware of the thing in your attachment and will try to use it *archetypally* in the new image. For example, if your attached image is boots, Midjourney will struggle if you ask for a boat or a hat in the shape of that boot. It will try to treat the boots as boots. *Repurposing objects against their archetype is not easy*. To do it, you’ll need to drop `--ow` to values well below the default of `100` and try some tricks.

</aside>

### 📦 Distinctive features work best with oref.

The more distinctive the features, the better the results. 

**✅ Attributes that work well with `--oref`:**

**Big signature features:** teal curly hair, pink sunglasses, floor-length trench coat, green backpack

**❌ Attributes that don’t work well with `--oref`:**

**Small or complex details:** a silver pendant necklace with eight small pyramid-shaped jewels, a leather jacket with the left sleeve missing that says "ZOOM" across the back, an undercut hairstyle with infinity symbols shaved onto the temples

<aside>
💡

Character or object *details* may still change due to randomness and the play of other style and vibe modifiers. To address this, explore anchoring or stabilizing important traits directly in the prompt.

</aside>

### Weight values of all kinds are important when using oref.

Explore combining ALL the transfer tools for richer results. And explore *removing or reducing* these very same things if you are struggling to make your oref appear “as-is” in the output.

| Tool | Result | How to Preserve Oref |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Style Reference `--sref` | *Bring in the style.* | Reduce `--sw` to preserve oref. |
| Omni Reference `--oref` | *Bring in the face or shape.* | Increase `--ow` to preserve oref. |
| `image prompt` | *Bring in the composition.* | Reduce `--iw` to preserve oref. |
| Profiles & Mood Boards `--p` | *Bring in a vibe.* | Reduce `--s` to preserve oref. |

### 📦 Different kinds of prompting do different things with oref.

There are three key ways to handle prompting with `--oref`. You can anchor the details (pose, expression, emotion, costume, props, scene, action) to maintain control, use a lighter touch and let `--oref` handle most of it, or provide just the scene and let `--oref` do ALL the work based on its semantic understanding of the contents of the reference image.

In all three cases, the prompt will blend with whatever attributes Midjourney identifies from the `--oref` image(s) you provide. Your prompt can anchor important details of your character or object, or it can be hands-off, allowing the `--oref` image(s) to guide the details fully.

### ⚓ Method #1: Anchoring / Stabilizing

*If you want to control the canvas, this is your method. This is when your prompt reinforces signature attributes from the reference image and explicitly poses the character or positions the object, aggressively if necessary. We recommend using the default value of `--ow` for people (100) but a low value of `--ow` for objects (20) if you are trying to repurpose shapes.* 

```
A portrait in the style of Teen Magazine photography. Jo is a young woman with blue curly hair, pink sunglasses, and a colorful scarf around her neck. She stands on a sidewalk on a busy city street. --oref https://cdn.midjourney.com/4b6da3fb-f752-40f5-9d20-c5a7697dffb9/0_3.png 

Surreal photography. Abigail stands in the forest wearing jeans, a t-shirt, and sandals. She wears a tall leather boot-shaped hat on her head, a hat made from a vintage boot, it's got laces, shaped like an old military engineering boot, on her head, as a boot-shaped hat. --oref https://s.mj.run/eAdna9ES5pM

```

### 👤 Method #2:  "Someone."

*If you want to give Midjourney more control, this is your method. This is when you write a “hands-off” prompt letting `--oref` do the work. Notice the prompt does NOT reinforce signature details. It just references the character or object generically. This allows Midjourney to blend and imagine more on its own. We recommend using a higher value of `--ow` for people or objects to reinforce details if you’re doing this kind of prompting.*

```
Someone waits in line at the bank.  --oref https://cdn.midjourney.com/4b6da3fb-f752-40f5-9d20-c5a7697dffb9/0_3.png --ow 400

Some stuff sits on the docks. --oref https://s.mj.run/eAdna9ES5pM --ow 1000
```

### 🖼️ Method #3: Scene-only.

A third approach is available if your `--oref` image already contains the pose and other desired details. You describe only the setting, surroundings, context, or background. Midjourney will blend the referenced character or object into the described scene. If results seem incoherent, try increasing the stylize (`--s`) value and/or experimental (`--exp`) values, e.g., `--s 800 --exp 80`

*Notice the prompt doesn't mention the character or object explicitly, trusting Midjourney to place it intelligently, relying on its semantic understanding of the reference image.*

```
An empty cornfield beneath an ominous stormy sky. --oref https://cdn.midjourney.com/4b6da3fb-f752-40f5-9d20-c5a7697dffb9/0_3.png --s 800 --exp 80 --ow 400
```

### 📦 Semantics, archetypes, and omni reference weight (`--ow` )

This takes a minute to understand. So bear with me.

When you feed Midjourney an image using `--oref`, it's not just copying pixels. It's actually trying to understand **what the hell it's looking at.** It's figuring out—*semantically*, that is—what makes a "chair" a chair or a "dog" a dog, instead of seeing just shapes, colors, and blobs.

So if your reference is a picture of a banana wearing sunglasses, Midjourney isn’t just seeing "yellow curved thing + dark plastic thingies." It’s trying to grasp the concept: “Oh, a *banana* wearing *sunglasses*.” 

Here’s evidence of the fact Midjourney has a semantic understanding of your attached image. Squint at the attachment. See that? I’ll give you a moment.

![Image provided by community member `colect`](attachment:65f6c945-e90c-4767-96c8-943bc7b92711:image.png)

Image provided by community member `colect`

The problem is that this semantic understanding can *trap* Midjourney into depicting only the best-known, least-original appearances of things. *Bananas don't wear sunglasses. Sunglasses aren't worn by bananas.* So, left to its own devices, Midjourney may just put a banana on the table next to some sunglasses. That's the archetype. Archetypes are powerful. And your prompt is probably fighting to do something non-archetypal. That's hard. That’s also art, right? That's why we have to drop `--ow` below 100, *to pry Midjourney's fingers off the archetype.*

The attributes Midjourney identifies from your reference image(s) blend with the prompt to create the final output. Control this transfer roughly using the `--ow N` parameter (weight), with values of `N` from `1-1000`. The default is `100`.

- ⬆️ Higher values (max `--ow 1000`) will create more archetypal adherence, which doesn’t mean more loyalty to the details of your image. It means more loyalty to the *semantic understanding* Midjourney has of your image.
- ➡️ A value of `--ow 100` (default) picks up roughly everything as-is, nice balance.
- ⬇️ Lower values (below 100) mean less archetypal adherence, so more creativity allowed.

### 📦 Aspect ratios matter with `--oref.`

1. **Size matters.** 
    
    **Make the object or person the correct size in your ref image. If you want it smaller, add more empty space around it first. Midjourney copies the size from your example.** 
    
    It’s best if the object or person in the reference image is already approximately the size you want it to be in the result. Midjourney struggles with sizes and proportions. Add space around your subject in the reference image to help it be smaller in the frame of the reference image if you want it to become smaller in the output. (You can do this in the Editor or with Zoom.)
    
    This is a good example of making objects and people “the right size”:
    
    ![image.png](attachment:be12c1f9-49bb-4979-9c9e-40f6ef2e53f6:image.png)

    Provided by community member `colect`
    
2. **Provide enough room on the canvas.** 
    
    **If your object is tall, choose a tall (portrait) aspect ratio. If your object is wide, choose a wide (landscape) aspect ratio.** 
    
    You’ll get better results if your aspect ratio gives Midjourney enough room. For example, a full body image of someone squashed into a square or landscape aspect ratio is more likely to cause proportion issues. If you need an image to be a certain aspect ratio we recommend a plan to crop it later, but in Midjourney *generate* it in the aspect ratio that creates the best image. 
    
3. **Use all the same aspect ratios to avoid black bars.**
    
    Strive to make sure all your external image references have the same aspect ratios. Where there’s a difference, you might get black bars like this.

### 📦  **Other important things to know about `--oref`.**

1. *What's the best type of image to use?*  Use images with clear, obvious attributes prominently shown. Simple, clear images often work best. Creating a library of reference images in various poses can help reinforce your text prompts.
2. *Do I have to use multiple images?* **No.** Generally, one clear reference image is enough. You can only use one `--oref` image at a time.
3. *Does my subject need to have a plain background?* **No.** Midjourney recognizes objects clearly against any background, provided they aren't hidden or camouflaged. The clearer your object, the better.
4. *Does my subject need to be shown alone?* Yes. Use images with only one distinct subject. If there are multiple subjects, use the prompt to draw out the desirable details.
5. *Can I use a reference sheet with multiple views or poses?* **Yes.** Reference sheets or turnaround images can work, but single, clear images often perform better. Test to see what works best for your project.
6. *Can I use any style of image as a reference?* **Yes.** But the style of your oref may be communicated to the output. Lower your `--ow.`
7. *How do I Pan, Zoom Out, or Edit an oref image? It appears I can’t?!*  You can! Use the Full Editor. Open the image from your Gallery into the Full Editor using the [Edit] button. Make sure the `--oref` and `--ow` parameters are deleted from the prompt or it will throw an error: `Omni-reference is not supported for this job type.` You can also download them and reintroduce them as an external upload to the web Editor.

### 📦 Troubleshooting `--oref`

### 1️⃣ I can't give the character or object a new pose, purpose, style, or details.

Lower the `--ow` value. Increase the detailing in the prompt.

### 2️⃣ I can't change my character's face specifically. The face is "locked."

That's correct. Midjourney preserves the face when using `--oref` at almost any value of `--ow,` often preserving attributes like beard and glasses. If you need to change the face more:

**Follow these steps:**

1. Use `/settings` to ensure you're on `[Low Variation]` mode and have `Remix` ON (not required on web).
2. Choose the image with the face you want to change. Expand it and select **[Vary Region]** (web: **[Edit]**).
3. In Editor mode, remove the `--oref` and `--ow` from the prompt.
4. Mask/select the face area and alter your prompt. For example, mask the beard area and add `clean-shaven`.

Once satisfied, do NOT re-add `--oref` to your prompt, or unwanted attributes will return.

### 3️⃣ Stuff looks janky or broken.

Midjourney sometimes struggles to position characters or objects coherently. For incoherent results (like odd cropping or backward heads), try increasing stylize (`--s`) to `800-1000`. You can also experiment with setting `--exp` to give another boost to coherency. 

### 4️⃣ Stuff is missing altogether.

If details are missing from your canvas, anchor it in the prompt.

### 6️⃣ It appears that I can’t Pan, Zoom Out, or Edit an image I made with oref?

You can! Use the Full Editor. Open the image from your Gallery into the Full Editor using the [Edit] button. Make sure the `--oref` and `--ow` parameters are deleted from the prompt or it will throw an error: `Omni-reference is not supported for this job type.` You can also download them and reintroduce them as an external upload to the web Editor.
